AI-Assisted Engineering

How to talk about using AI in your work — for "AI prompting" rounds & "how do you use AI?" questions
🎧 Interview prep · reusable for Cresta / Google / any AI-enabled role

What this round is really testing

An "AI prompting" / "how do you use AI" round is not testing whether you know magic incantations. It's testing whether you use AI with judgment — the exact phrase these JDs use is "know when AI output should be trusted, verified, or overridden." They want someone who is faster because of AI but still owns the correctness.

Your reframe (important): you told yourself "I just talk to it like a person, no method." That's actually the right approach — giving context and iterating is exactly how effective prompting works. You're not missing a method; you just need the language to describe what you already do, and to emphasize the verify/judgment part.

The one-liner to anchor on

"I treat AI like a very fast junior engineer I'm directing. I give it context, have it do the legwork — reading code, drafting, correlating — and iterate. But the judgment is mine: I always verify its output against ground truth, and I've caught plenty of confident-but-wrong answers. AI accelerates the work; I own the outcome."

That single paragraph hits everything they want: speed from AI, you direct it, you verify, you own correctness. Memorize the shape, not the words.

My method — 4 moves (this IS your "prompting technique")

You already do these. Naming them turns "I just chat with it" into a described methodology.

1
Give context like briefing a new teammate. Don't drop a one-liner. Give the background, the goal, the constraints, the relevant files — the same brief you'd give a person joining the problem. Better context in → better output.
2
Make it show evidence, not just answers. For anything factual, I have it pull the actual code / logs / source to back a claim — a data-flow from the real code, not a plausible guess. "Show me where" beats "tell me what."
3
Iterate — first output is a draft. I treat the first answer as a starting point, then refine: "that's wrong here, because…", "now handle this edge case", "tighten this". It's a conversation, not a one-shot.
4
Verify against ground truth — I own the call. I check the output against reality — run it, diff it against logs, compare to a known-good case. The diagnostic judgment (what to suspect, how to verify each step) stays mine. This is the move they care about most.

The framing that lands: "Think of me as the tech lead and AI as a member I'm directing — I set the direction and make every call; AI accelerates the parts I'd otherwise do slowly, like reading an unfamiliar codebase. I own the outcome either way."

Real stories — you have strong ones, use them

Don't speak in the abstract. These are things you actually did; each shows the method + judgment. (Keep them generalized — no internal system names / secrets.)

① The distributed-system bug others were stuck on for weeks

"I root-caused a failure a team had been stuck on for weeks. My method: I drove an AI agent to pull the relevant code and produce a data-flow diagram backed by code evidence — not guesses. Then I validated that model against real logs, stage by stage, until I found the one stage that was silently failing. The AI accelerated the reading and mapping; the judgment — what to suspect, how to verify each stage — was mine. I left the model behind as a runbook."

Shows: context + evidence (move 2) + verify against logs (move 4) + you own the diagnosis. Your single best AI story.

② Ramping into an unfamiliar system fast

"Dropped into a system I'd never touched, two days before a deadline. I fed an AI agent the codebase, tickets, and chat history to generate an architecture summary and trace service relationships — got from zero to a working mental model in a day instead of a week. Then I did the actual root-causing myself."

Shows: AI for the legwork (reading/summarizing) so you spend energy on the hard part.

③ Full-stack fix on an unfamiliar codebase, solo

"I directed AI to help me trace an unfamiliar full-stack flow and draft the verification steps, then I drove the end-to-end testing myself and confirmed each fix. I set what to trace and what 'correct' meant; AI accelerated the slow parts. Tech lead directing a member — I own the result."

④ Verify-before-acting (judgment story)

"An automated security finding flagged an old credential. Instead of blindly rotating it, I used the audit trail to find what actually used it — it turned out to be an automation, not a person — then took the reversible action. AI/tools accelerate the investigation, but I decide the safe move based on verified evidence, not the alert's face value."

Shows: judgment + "don't trust the surface" — exactly the "when to verify/override" theme.

Why this matters to them: the JDs literally say "maintain rigor, originality, and judgment that do not come from AI." This answer proves you have exactly that — you're not someone who blindly ships AI output.

Likely questions & how to answer

Q: "How do you use AI in your day-to-day work?"

Lead with the one-liner (fast junior engineer I direct), then the 4 moves briefly, then one concrete story (the distributed-system bug). End on "the judgment is mine." Don't list tools — describe the method.

Q: "Walk me through how you'd prompt AI to solve [X]."

Narrate the 4 moves on the actual problem: "First I'd give it context — the goal, constraints, relevant code. Then have it pull evidence rather than guess. I'd iterate on the draft. And I'd verify the result against [logs/tests/known-good] before trusting it." Show the loop, not a single magic prompt.

Q: "When has AI been wrong, and how did you catch it?"

The override examples — a hallucinated API, a fix missing an edge case. "That's why I verify anything factual against the real source." This is a strength question disguised as a gotcha — you want them to ask it.

Q: "How is your work different from just asking ChatGPT?"

"I set the direction and make every call — what to investigate, what 'correct' means, how to verify. AI accelerates the slow legwork; it doesn't decide anything. Tech lead directing a member. I own the outcome, including when the AI is wrong."

Q: "Doesn't relying on AI make engineers weaker?"

"It shifts where the skill is. Less time memorizing API surface, more on judgment, design, and verification — which is where the value always was. The risk is people who ship AI output without understanding it; I use it to go faster on things I could do slower, not to skip understanding."

Closing frame (fits an AI company like Cresta): "AI-assisted is just how I work now — I've used it to crack bugs teams were stuck on, ramp into unfamiliar systems in days, and turn one-off debugging into reusable runbooks. But I treat it as acceleration with mandatory verification, never as a replacement for owning the correctness."
