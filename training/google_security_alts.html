What ALTS is, in one paragraph

ALTS is Google's in-house equivalent of "mutual TLS for everything internal." It's the layer that authenticates both ends of a connection and encrypts the traffic for the roughly 1010 RPCs per second flowing between Google's microservices. It is zero-configuration: any workload scheduled in production has its RPCs secured by default — an engineer writing a service does nothing to get mutual authentication and encryption. Development began in 2007, because TLS at the time bundled legacy protocol baggage that didn't meet Google's minimum security bar.

Mental model: ALTS is to internal service traffic what HTTPS is to the public web — but built around service identity instead of hostnames, serialized with Protocol Buffers instead of X.509/ASN.1, and provisioned automatically by the cluster scheduler instead of by a human buying a certificate.

Where it's used

  • Every internal RPC. Service A calling service B inside Google is an ALTS-protected channel. It's the substrate the entire microservice fleet sits on.
  • Foundation for BeyondProd. ALTS provides the service-identity + mutual-auth primitives that Google's "zero-trust for production" model (BeyondProd) builds on. See the BeyondProd deep-dive.
  • Cloud KMS internals. Cloud KMS secures its own client↔server RPC via ALTS (customer↔GFE uses TLS). See KMS deep-dive.
  • Enables elasticity. Because identity isn't bound to a host, workloads can be replicated, load-balanced, and rescheduled across machines without re-issuing host certs.

Trust model: identity, not hostname

Every network entity — a user, a machine, or a workload — has an identity embedded in its ALTS certificates, and communications are mutually authenticated by identity. Production services run as production entities (managed by SREs); dev builds run as test entities, and production configs are set not to trust dev entities. This is what makes replication trivial: five replicas of a service all present the same identity, so a caller doesn't care which host or IP answered.

The certificate chain & root of trust

Three certificate types make up the system:

  1. Master certificate — signed by a remote Signing Service; contains a public key (e.g. RSA) and acts as a constrained intermediate CA. Issued to machines and to schedulers (Borgmaster).
  2. Handshake certificate — created and signed locally by the master private key; carries handshake parameters (static Diffie-Hellman params, ciphers). Short-lived.
  3. Resumption key — a secret that encrypts resumption tickets, indexed by a Resumption Identifier (ID_R) shared by workloads with the same identity in the same datacenter cell.

The Signing Service verification keys are the root of trust, pre-installed on all Google production and corporate machines. On machines with a Titan chip, trust can be rooted in that hardware.

Bootstrapping insight: workload credentials are delivered over an ALTS channel that the machine credential already secured. The machine identity (rooted in hardware/secure-boot) is the anchor that lets the cluster hand a fresh identity to a brand-new workload safely.

The server uses HKDF (extract/expand, RFC-5869) to derive three secrets from the DH result:

  • M — the record-protocol key (encrypts subsequent traffic)
  • R — the resumption secret
  • A — the authenticator secret (an HMAC over a predefined bit string proves possession)

Record protocol (ALTSRP) & the boundary-based encryption choice

Once M is established, traffic is framed (32-bit Length, 32-bit Type, Payload; max frame ~1MB). The clever bit is mode selection by physical boundary:

Where the traffic flowsModeMeaning
Within a Google-controlled physical boundaryAES-128-GMAC / VMAC (integrity-only)Payload in plaintext + a cryptographic tag — cheaper, upgradable by data sensitivity
Crossing a boundary / over the WANAES-128-GCM / VCM (authenticated encryption)Automatically upgraded to full confidentiality + integrity

Both peers keep request/response counters (never sent on the wire) and drop out-of-order or repeated frames.

Why integrity-only inside a datacenter? If you already trust the physical boundary, you pay only for tamper-detection, not encryption — a deliberate performance/security trade at 1010 RPCs/sec. The moment traffic leaves that boundary, ALTS transparently upgrades to authenticated encryption.

Session resumption & revocation

Resumption avoids repeating expensive asymmetric ops. Tickets are indexed by ID_R and symmetrically encrypted. Because ID_R ties to identity, not a specific connection, a client can resume with a server it never previously contacted — extremely useful behind load balancers. On resumption, R derives fresh M′/R′/A′ (R itself is never reused).

Revocation: certificates are invalidated by expiration or via a Certificate Revocation List (CRL). Human certs expire daily; production machine certs often omit timestamps (to avoid clock-sync outages) and rely on the CRL as source of truth. A revocation ID is 64-bit (8-bit category + 56-bit id). The CRL DB is hundreds of MB but compresses to a few MB and is distributed to every machine.

Policy enforcement happens at verification time (not issuance): the ALTS policy lists which issuers may issue which cert categories for which identities, enabling flexible per-cluster policies while blocking impersonation by unauthorized issuers.

Why Google built it this way

Why not just use TLS?

  • 2007 TLS carried legacy baggage that fell below Google's minimum security standards. Rather than fight the interop-driven complexity, Google built a narrower protocol it fully controlled.
  • Simplicity = auditability. A simpler protocol with Protobuf serialization is far easier to audit by inspection and fuzzing than X.509/ASN.1 parsing (historically a rich source of CVEs).
  • Full control of the endpoints. Google owns both ends of every internal connection, so it doesn't need the internet's flexibility — it can standardize on strong choices.

Why identity instead of hostname?

In a fleet where workloads are constantly rescheduled, replicated, and load-balanced, binding trust to a hostname is a nightmare — every move needs a new cert. Binding to a service identity means N replicas share one identity and can move freely. This single decision is what makes Google's elasticity and BeyondProd's "trust the workload, not the network location" model possible.

Why frequent rotation instead of PFS-by-default?

PFS is supported but not the default; instead ALTS rotates certificates very frequently (handshake certs every few hours, human certs daily). At Google's scale, aggressive rotation bounds the exposure window of any compromised key while avoiding the per-connection cost of always-on PFS. When PFS is enabled, it also covers resumed sessions.

Why no 0-RTT?

Deliberately excluded. Google's RPC connections are long-lived, so 0-RTT's extra complexity and weaker security guarantees weren't worth it — the handshake cost amortizes across a long-lived connection.

Scale & throughput — making crypto free enough to be always-on

ALTS protects ~1010 RPCs/sec, so the whole design is bent toward making per-RPC security cost almost nothing. The whitepaper frames these as security choices, but they're equally throughput choices:

  • Session resumption skips asymmetric crypto. The expensive part of a handshake is the public-key op. Resumption tickets (indexed by ID_R) re-key with cheap symmetric ops; because ID_R ties to identity not connection, a client can even resume with a server it never talked to — huge behind load balancers.
  • Long-lived connections amortize the handshake. The 1-RTT handshake is paid once and spread over millions of messages. (Also why 0-RTT was skipped — not worth it for long-lived channels.)
  • Boundary-aware modes save CPU. Inside a trusted physical boundary, integrity-only (GMAC/VMAC) avoids full-encryption cost; only cross-boundary traffic pays for AEAD. Spend crypto cycles only where the threat is.
  • Local CRL copies. The revocation list compresses to a few MB and sits on every machine, so a handshake checks revocation locally — no per-connection call to a central service.
  • Rotation instead of always-on PFS. Frequent cert rotation gives forward-secrecy-like properties without PFS's per-connection asymmetric cost by default.

The scaling lesson: to make a security check "always on" fleet-wide, drive its marginal cost toward zero — cache/resume to skip the asymmetric step, amortize setup over long-lived connections, and pay for the strongest protection only where the threat model demands it.

Hard problems & deliberate trade-offs

Bootstrapping trust for a brand-new workload

A freshly scheduled workload has no identity yet. The resolution: the machine's credential (anchored in secure boot / Titan) secures the channel over which Borgmaster delivers the workload's credential. Trust is layered from hardware → machine → workload.

Revocation at fleet scale without clock dependence

Timestamps on machine certs would make the fleet vulnerable to clock-sync outages, so those certs often omit expiry and rely on the CRL. The challenge becomes distributing a hundreds-of-MB revocation database everywhere — solved by compression (to a few MB) plus local copies checked during each handshake.

Accepting KCI susceptibility on purpose

ALTS is, by design, susceptible to Key Compromise Impersonation (KCI) attacks — an accepted trade-off given the resumption threat model. A KCI-resistant variant exists but is only used where resumption isn't needed. This is a great example of a security system making an explicit, documented trade rather than chasing theoretical purity.

Handshake privacy

Handshake messages aren't encrypted, so peer identities aren't hidden during the handshake — acceptable inside a controlled network, and a cost of the 1-RTT simplicity.

Performance at 1010 RPC/s

The integrity-only-inside-the-boundary mode, session resumption (skip asymmetric ops), and long-lived connections are all in service of one thing: making per-RPC crypto cheap enough to be on by default for the entire fleet.

Interview angle

Why this matters for a Security Backend / Silicon role: ALTS is the connective tissue of Google's production security. If the team does service security, key/identity infrastructure, or anything touching the hardware root of trust, ALTS is the vocabulary they think in. You don't need to have built ALTS — you need to show you understand the principles and can reason about the trade-offs.

What the team likely does (so you can speak their language)

  • Service identity / credential issuance & rotation infrastructure (the CA, Signing Service, CRL side).
  • Rooting service identity in hardware (Titan) — the "machine credential only decryptable if secure boot passed" flow is exactly the Silicon ↔ Security seam.
  • Making mutual auth zero-config and cheap enough to be always-on.

Likely interview questions & how to approach them

Q: "Design a service-to-service authentication system for a large microservice fleet."

Lead with identity over hostname, mutual auth by default, and credential provisioning through the scheduler. Bring up rotation-vs-PFS trade-off, and rooting machine identity in hardware. Mention revocation (CRL) and policy-at-verification-time.

Q: "Why not just use mutual TLS?"

Talk about simplicity/auditability, Protobuf vs X.509/ASN.1 parsing risk, identity-vs-hostname for elasticity, and controlling both endpoints. Show you know TLS well enough to know when to deviate from it.

Q: "How would you bound the blast radius of a leaked credential?"

Frequent rotation (short-lived handshake certs), CRL-based revocation, hardware-rooted machine identity so a stolen software cred is useless off its bound machine, and identity policy enforced at verification.

Q: "How do you make crypto cheap enough to be always-on?"

Boundary-aware modes (integrity-only inside trusted physical boundary; upgrade to AEAD crossing it), session resumption to skip asymmetric ops, long-lived connections amortizing the handshake.

Closing move in an interview: connect ALTS to the bigger picture — "ALTS gives you identity + mutual auth; BeyondProd builds authorization and code-provenance on top; Titan roots the machine identity in hardware; KMS protects the data those services touch. They're one coherent zero-trust story." Showing you see the system of systems is what distinguishes a senior/staff-level answer.
