What Zanzibar is, in one paragraph

Zanzibar is Google's global authorization system — the service that answers one deceptively simple question: "Does user U have relation R to object O?" (e.g. "can Alice view this Doc?"). Instead of every product building its own permission logic, Calendar, Cloud, Drive, Maps, Photos, and YouTube all delegate that decision to Zanzibar. It stores >2 trillion access-control lists (ACLs) and serves >10 million authorization checks per second at <10 ms p95, with >99.999% availability over 3 years.

Why this doc exists: the other four systems (Titan, ALTS, BeyondProd, KMS) are security whitepapers — they explain how to be secure, not how to scale a backend. Zanzibar is the one that's explicitly both: a security system whose paper is largely about how to serve authorization at planet scale with strong consistency. That makes it the best single artifact for a "security backend" interview.

Where it's used

  • The permission check behind the product. Sharing a Doc, opening a Photos album, viewing a private video — each triggers a Zanzibar Check. A single search query can fan out to tens to hundreds of checks (one per candidate result).
  • The blueprint for the industry. Zanzibar directly inspired open-source clones — Auth0/Okta FGA, OpenFGA, SpiceDB, Airbnb's Himeji, Carta's AuthZ. "ReBAC" (relationship-based access control) as a category comes from this paper.
  • Complements the stack. ALTS answers "which service is calling?"; BeyondProd's EUC ticket answers "on whose behalf?"; Zanzibar answers "is that user actually allowed to touch this object?"

The four goals it was built to hit (all at once)

GoalWhat it means
CorrectnessRespect the causal order of ACL changes — never apply a stale ACL to new content (the "new enemy problem")
FlexibilityOne data model + config language rich enough for every Google product's permission scheme
Low latencyChecks sit in the critical path of user interactions; tail latency especially matters (hundreds of checks per action)
Scale + availabilityBillions of objects, billions of users, deployed globally near clients

The hard part is that these fight each other — strong consistency usually costs latency and availability. Most of the design is about having all four without trading one away.

The data model: relation tuples

Everything in Zanzibar is a relation tuple — one tiny fact about who relates to what. The trick to reading it: it's just a sentence — subject → verb → object, but written back-to-front.

More examples — read each one right-to-left as a sentence:

doc:readme#owner@user:alice          "alice is the owner of doc:readme"
folder:proj#parent@folder:root       "folder:proj's parent is folder:root"
doc:readme#viewer@group:eng#member   "every member of group:eng can view doc:readme"

The one clever bit: a userset (that third line)

Usually the "who" is a single person (user:alice). But it can also be a whole group, written group:eng#member — which itself is just "the members of group:eng." So instead of writing one tuple per person, you point at the group once:

  • group:eng#member@user:bob — "bob is a member of group:eng"
  • doc:readme#viewer@group:eng#member — "anyone who is a member of group:eng can view doc:readme"

Add bob to the group, and he can now view the doc — without touching the doc's tuples at all. And because a group can point at another group, nested groups just work. This is the key insight: a group is nothing special — it's the exact same tuple structure as a permission. ("ACLs and groups are the same data structure.")

Why tuples instead of a permissions blob per object? One uniform little fact-format serves every product, can be indexed generically, and lets Zanzibar reason about the ordering of each individual change (which matters for the "new enemy problem" below). Think of it like OAuth vs Zanzibar: "if OAuth is handing someone a key, Zanzibar is deciding which rooms that key opens."

Userset rewrite rules — computed permissions

Stored tuples don't capture rules like "editors can also view." Those live in the namespace config as userset rewrite rules, evaluated at check time into a boolean expression tree:

RuleMeaning
_thisThe tuples stored directly for this relation
computed_userset"also include another relation on the same object" (e.g. viewer ⊇ editor ⊇ owner)
tuple_to_usersetThe powerful one: inheritance — "viewer of this doc = viewer of its parent folder." Follows a tuple to another object, then evaluates a relation there.
union / intersection / exclusionBoolean combination of the above

So "can Alice view doc:readme?" becomes: check direct viewers ∪ editors ∪ owners ∪ (viewers of the parent folder) … — a tree Zanzibar evaluates concurrently.

The "new enemy problem" — what it is, and why it's scary

This is the one correctness bug that makes a permission system dangerous. The name says it: someone you just kicked out becomes a "new enemy" who can still see new secrets, because a permission change and a content change got applied out of order.

Why does a "stale copy" even exist? Because Zanzibar is global — the ACL lives in many data centers, and a change takes a moment to spread everywhere. During that gap, a nearby replica still has the old permissions. If a check reads that replica, it uses yesterday's rules on today's content. (Same bug, second flavor: even ordering aside, evaluating a check at a stale moment applies an old ACL to new content.)

The fix: zookies — a "freshness receipt"

A zookie is Zanzibar's consistency token. The analogy that makes it click: it's a timestamped receipt.

The receipt travels with the content. Later, any check hands the receipt back and says "give me an answer at least this fresh." So a check can never be evaluated against permissions older than the content it's guarding — the new-enemy window is closed. (Zookies are opaque byte strings, so clients can't forge a timestamp.) Under the hood this rides on Spanner and its TrueTime clock, which hands out globally-agreed timestamps — that's what makes "time T" mean the same thing in every data center.

Why this doesn't cost a slow global round-trip

Here's the clever part, spelled out. The naïve way to be correct is "always read the very latest permissions everywhere" — but that forces a slow cross-region sync on every single check. Zanzibar avoids it because the zookie only demands "at least this fresh," not "the absolute newest."

That one word — at least — is the whole trick. It splits checks into two buckets:

BucketWhat it needsWhere it's servedSpeed
"Safe" (zookie older than ~10s)Any replica already has data this freshA nearby local replica — no cross-region tripFast (single-digit ms)
"Recent" (zookie within ~10s)Might need the freshest dataMay take a cross-region round-tripSlower (~tens of ms)

The payoff: "safe" checks outnumber "recent" ones by roughly 100 : 1 — by design. The vast majority of checks are served from fast local data and are still provably correct, because the zookie guarantees they're fresh enough. Only the rare just-changed case pays for a global trip. That's how Zanzibar gets strong consistency and low latency instead of trading one for the other.

From a sample 7-day window: Check ≈ 8.2M QPS, Expand ≈ 760K, Write ≈ 25K — reads outnumber writes by ~two orders of magnitude. Clusters range from <100 to >1,000 servers (median ~500), sized to regional load. This is the read-heavy, latency-critical, globally-distributed workload profile — and every technique below exists to serve it.

1 · Consistency that doesn't cost a global round-trip

The zookie's "at-least-as-fresh" semantics let Zanzibar evaluate most checks against locally-replicated, slightly-stale snapshots instead of forcing every check to read the global latest (which would need high-latency cross-region synchronization). Each server also tracks safe staleness bounds and even runs a two-proportion z-test before increasing the default staleness — pushing more traffic onto local data without violating correctness.

2 · Leopard — precomputing the expensive part

The costly operation is deeply/widely nested group membership ("is Alice in a group, that's in a group, that's in a group … that can view this?"). Recursive pointer-chasing at check time is slow. Leopard is a specialized index that:

  • Represents sets as (T, s, e) tuples (type, set-id, element-id) and flattens group-to-group paths so reachability is a set lookup, not a traversal.
  • Stores them in skip-list-style structures so union and intersection across large sets are efficient (intersecting A and B skips ahead instead of scanning).
  • Is kept fresh by consuming the Watch stream of tuple changes — an offline builder + online serving split, shards served from memory.

The pattern: denormalize the expensive read (nested reachability) into a precomputed index, and keep it eventually-fresh via a change stream — without denormalizing the primary data (which stays normalized in Spanner for consistency).

3 · Hot-spot handling — the real scaling lever

ACL workloads are bursty: a search query checks many results that share common groups; a viral object draws a flood of checks. Zanzibar's defenses:

  • Distributed cache across the cluster. Cache entries are distributed with consistent hashing; internal RPCs are forwarded by a key so the same check/read lands on the same server (cache trees). Snapshot timestamp is part of the cache key, so caching never breaks consistency.
  • Lock table (single-flight). When many identical requests arrive before the cache is warm (a "flash hot spot"), a per-server lock table tracks outstanding reads/checks so only one does the work and the rest wait for the result — collapsing a thundering herd into one query.
  • Read-and-cache-all for hot objects. A popular object hit by many checks-for-different-users would hammer the storage server; Zanzibar detects hot objects (by counting outstanding reads) and reads+caches all its tuples at once.
  • Slicer redistributes hot keys across servers dynamically.

4 · Tail latency — request hedging

For calls to Spanner and Leopard, Zanzibar uses request hedging: send the same request to multiple replicas, take the first response, cancel the rest. To avoid doubling load, it sends one request first and only fires hedged copies once the first is known to be slow — using a per-server delay estimator that computes an Nth-percentile latency from recent measurements. It also places ≥2 replicas near every Zanzibar server. Hedging requires similar-cost requests, so evaluation is structured to keep sub-requests comparable.

Why hedging matters: at <10ms p95 across >10M QPS, a few slow replicas would wreck the tail. Hedging trades a little extra load for a dramatically tighter tail — the classic "tail at scale" technique.

5 · Concurrency & cancellation

All leaf nodes of the boolean check tree are evaluated concurrently. Because a boolean result can short-circuit (one true in a union is enough), indirect sub-checks are frequently cancelled once the answer is known — though Zanzibar delays eager cancellation when other requests are waiting on the same in-flight work, so it doesn't throw away a result someone else needs.

Why this design

Why centralize authorization at all?

  • Consistency of a hard problem. Authorization bugs are privacy incidents. Solving ordering/staleness once, correctly, beats every team reinventing it (and getting the "new enemy problem" wrong).
  • Uniformity. One data model + config language means tooling, auditing, and the Leopard index work for every client.
  • Operational leverage. One system to run at 5-nines instead of dozens of bespoke permission stores.

Why relationship tuples (ReBAC) instead of roles (RBAC)?

Real products are graphs: docs in folders, folders shared with groups, groups nested in groups. Roles (RBAC) can't express "viewer of a doc = viewer of its parent folder" cleanly. Modeling permissions as relationships + rewrite rules captures inheritance and nesting directly — which is exactly why the whole industry copied this model (OpenFGA, SpiceDB, etc.).

Why lean on Spanner + zookies rather than "always read latest"?

Always reading the freshest ACL would give correctness trivially — but at the cost of a global synchronization round-trip on every check, destroying latency and availability. Zookies invert it: capture just enough ordering (a timestamp bound tied to content) so that slightly stale local reads are provably safe. It's the minimal consistency needed, and no more.

Why normalized storage + a separate index?

Denormalizing ACLs into storage would speed reads but make consistent, ordered updates a nightmare. Keeping the source of truth normalized in Spanner (for correctness) and pushing denormalization into a separately-maintained, eventually-fresh index (Leopard, for speed) cleanly separates the two concerns.

Hard problems

The new enemy problem

The defining correctness challenge: never let a removed user see new content, and never apply a stale ACL to fresh content. Solved by zookies + Spanner external consistency — but note it requires client cooperation (clients must store the zookie with their content and send it back). A client that skips zookies gets default staleness, not ordering guarantees.

Deeply nested groups

Group-in-group-in-group membership can make a single check traverse a huge subgraph. Leopard's flattened reachability index is the answer, but it introduces its own freshness challenge (kept current via the Watch stream), and a consistency gap: Leopard can't always evaluate at a perfectly fresh snapshot, so it's used where its staleness is acceptable.

Hot spots & the thundering herd

A viral object or a popular shared group can concentrate enormous load on a few tuples. Without the distributed cache + lock table + hot-object read-all + Slicer, these would overload individual storage servers. Much of the engineering is specifically about flattening bursty, skewed load.

Tail latency at 5-nines

Being in the critical path of user actions at >10M QPS means the p95/p99 tail is the product. Hedging, concurrent tree evaluation, local snapshots, and caching all exist to keep the tail under 10ms while maintaining >99.999% availability.

Interview angle

Why this is the highest-ROI system to know: it's the one paper that is simultaneously a security system and a large-scale backend system. For a Security Backend role, being able to whiteboard "design a global authorization service" using Zanzibar's ideas demonstrates both domains at once — which is exactly the intersection the role sits at.

Likely interview questions

Q: "Design a global authorization / permissions service."

This is the Zanzibar question. Lead with the relation-tuple model (object#relation@user, usersets for groups), rewrite rules for inheritance (tuple_to_userset), the Check API, then consistency (zookies + a Spanner-like externally-consistent store to prevent the "new enemy problem"), then scale (local snapshots, a Leopard-style flattening index for nested groups, distributed cache + lock table for hot spots, hedging for tail latency).

Q: "How do you keep a permission check correct when ACLs and content change concurrently?"

The new-enemy problem. Store ACLs in an externally-consistent DB with global timestamps; issue a consistency token (zookie) tied to content version; evaluate checks at a snapshot at least as fresh as the token. Explain why "always read latest" is correct but too slow, and why "at-least-as-fresh" is the right minimal guarantee.

Q: "A single object is getting a flood of permission checks — how do you not fall over?"

Distributed cache with consistent-hash forwarding, per-server lock table to single-flight duplicate checks, detect hot objects and read+cache all their tuples at once, Slicer to spread hot keys. Snapshot in the cache key to stay consistent.

Q: "How do you get <10ms p95 across millions of QPS globally?"

Serve from local replicated snapshots (zookie makes it safe), precompute nested membership (Leopard), evaluate the check tree concurrently with short-circuit cancellation, and hedge slow backend calls. Availability from replication across 30+ regions.

System-of-systems close: "ALTS proves which service is calling; BeyondProd's EUC ticket carries which user it's acting for; Zanzibar decides whether that user is actually allowed to touch the object — and does it at 10M QPS with <10ms tail by trading 'always fresh' for 'provably fresh enough.' It's where security correctness and backend scale meet." That framing shows you see the whole picture, not just one paper.
